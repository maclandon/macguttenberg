How pelvic floor PT works — an interactive guide through anatomy, assessment, and treatment

What is the pelvic floor?

The pelvic floor is a group of muscles that sit at the base of the pelvis like a hammock. They support the bladder, bowel, and uterus, and play a role in continence, sexual function, core stability, and breathing.

When these muscles are working well, they're mostly invisible — you don't think about them. When they're not, they make themselves known.

Hypertonic: the too-tight floor

A hypertonic pelvic floor is one that can't fully relax. The muscles stay in a state of chronic contraction — not because of strength, but because of guarding, stress, or learned tension patterns.

This is often the root cause of pelvic pain, pain during sex, urgency, and difficulty with bowel movements. More kegels will make it worse.

Hypotonic: the too-weak floor

A hypotonic pelvic floor lacks the strength or coordination to generate adequate force on demand. It can't close quickly enough when you cough, jump, or sneeze — leading to leaking.

This is where kegels are genuinely helpful, when done correctly with a full release between each rep.

A weak floor needs strength + timing — not just squeezing.

The breath–pelvic floor link

Your diaphragm and pelvic floor move together on every breath. On an inhale, the diaphragm descends and the pelvic floor gently drops. On an exhale, both recoil upward.

When breathing is shallow or the core is chronically braced, this rhythm breaks down — and the pelvic floor loses its natural release cycle.

What treatment involves

Pelvic floor PT is never one-size-fits-all. Treatment is built around what your specific floor needs — which requires an assessment first.

Sessions typically include a combination of manual therapy, breathing and nervous system work, movement re-education, and a home program.

Most people see meaningful progress within 4–8 sessions.
