My Story

I got here by fixing what wasn't working.

My path into design and digital systems didn't start in an agency or a design school. It started in a Long Island tax office, watching a $500K-a-year business operate entirely on paper, cash, and good intentions.

I'd come up through business operations and digital media — learning how organizations actually function, where the friction is, and what the gap between "how it should work" and "how it actually works" looks like in practice. That background shaped everything about how I approach design: I don't design things that look good. I design things that work.

When I rebuilt that tax practice from scratch — digital intake, online payments, a client portal, automated workflows — something clicked. The design decisions I was making weren't aesthetic. They were operational. Every screen had to earn its place by reducing friction or eliminating a manual step. That constraint made the work better.

Since then I've applied that same framework to e-commerce brands, service businesses, and solo operators across the country. The tools change. The discipline doesn't. Build systems that work without you. Remove every step that doesn't need to exist. Make it obvious what the user should do next.

I'm based in New York City and work remotely with businesses across the country. I don't subcontract, I don't hand off to a team, and I don't disappear after the kickoff call. I get in, understand your operation, and build something that actually runs.

Design Philosophy

Design that earns its place.

I didn't come to design through aesthetics. I came through operations — through watching businesses struggle with systems that looked fine but didn't work. That experience shaped a philosophy that's uncomfortable for designers who lead with visuals: every design decision should make something easier, faster, or less ambiguous for the person using it. If it doesn't do that, it shouldn't be there.

01
Clarity over cleverness.
The most important thing a design can do is make the next step obvious. Clever layouts, surprising interactions, unusual patterns — these are tools, not goals. If a user has to think about what to do next, the design has failed. I default to the obvious choice every time, and only deviate when there's a real reason.
02
Systems over surfaces.
A beautiful homepage that leads to a broken intake form is a net negative. I think about the full system — every touchpoint a user encounters from first visit to repeat customer. The weakest link determines the experience. I fix the whole chain, not just the visible parts.
03
Build for the operator, not just the user.
A system that delights the customer but exhausts the person running it isn't a good system. I design for both sides of the transaction. The client portal needs to be easy to use. It also needs to cut Mark's admin time by 50%. Both matter. I don't treat operational efficiency as a nice-to-have.
04
Constraints are a feature.
Most of my clients don't have enterprise budgets or six-month timelines. They need something that works in 30 days with the tools they already have. That constraint is clarifying. It forces prioritization. It eliminates the nice-to-haves. The result is usually better than what an unconstrained process would have produced.
